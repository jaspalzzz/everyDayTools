How to audit a California final paycheck
California's final-paycheck rule is a timing rule first and a documentation issue second. Terminations are due immediately on the day of termination, while resignations are due within 72 hours if no notice given; immediately if 72+ hours notice was provided. That makes the separation date, the reason employment ended, and the next regular payday especially important. Nearby states such as Alaska, Arizona, Colorado may use different triggers, so a regional payroll policy can be wrong for California.
- Final pay stub, direct-deposit timestamp, or mailed-check postmark.
- Any written demand sent before filing a wage claim.
- Last day worked and the date employment officially ended.
- Whether the separation was a resignation, layoff, discharge, or mutual end date.

What must be included in your final paycheck
A California final paycheck is only complete when it includes each of these:
- ✓Earned bonus amounts that have vested under the written plan
- ✓Accrued PTO or vacation only when the state rule or employer policy requires payout
- ✓All earned wages through your last day (hourly or salary)
- ✓Overtime pay owed for hours worked
Whether accrued vacation or PTO must be included depends on California's PTO payout law. See California PTO payout rules →
If your employer pays late
- 1. Consider a civil lawsuit where penalties, interest, or attorney fees are available.
- 2. Keep the final pay stub and payment timestamp with the demand letter.
- 3. Document your last day worked and what you are owed.
- 4. Confirm whether the deadline changed because you resigned rather than being terminated.
Frequently asked questions
How long does my employer have to give me my final paycheck in California?
In California, the deadline depends on how your employment ended. If your employer terminated you, your final paycheck is due immediately on the day of termination. If you resigned voluntarily, it is due within 72 hours if no notice given; immediately if 72+ hours notice was provided. These are minimum legal deadlines — your employer can pay sooner, but not later.
What must be included in my final paycheck in California?
A lawful California final paycheck includes all wages through your last day worked, any overtime, and commissions/bonuses that have vested and can be calculated. Whether unused PTO is added depends on California's PTO payout law. Only legally authorized deductions (tax, garnishments) are permitted.
What can I do if my employer is late with my final paycheck in California?
If your employer misses the final paycheck deadline in California, you can file a wage claim with the California Department of Labor (https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/). You may also have the right to file a civil lawsuit. Many states allow you to recover the unpaid wages plus penalties and attorney fees. Document everything: your last day, what you were owed, and all communications with your employer.
Can my employer deduct money from my final paycheck in California?
In California, a final paycheck can only be reduced by legally mandated amounts (tax, garnishment) or deductions you signed off on in writing. Docking pay to recover company property or an advance, absent that authorization or a court process, violates wage law.
Does California have penalties for employers who pay the final paycheck late?
There are remedies in California for a late final paycheck. Depending on circumstances you may claim penalties, liquidated damages, or interest on top of the unpaid wages. The California Department of Labor (https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/) can investigate and order payment, and you retain the right to sue.
