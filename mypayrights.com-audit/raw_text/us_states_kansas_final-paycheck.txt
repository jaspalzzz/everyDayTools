How to audit a Kansas final paycheck
Kansas's final-paycheck rule is a timing rule first and a documentation issue second. The same deadline applies whether the worker was terminated or resigned: next regular payday. That makes the separation date, the reason employment ended, and the next regular payday especially important. Nearby states such as Illinois, Indiana, Iowa may use different triggers, so a regional payroll policy can be wrong for Kansas.
- Last day worked and the date employment officially ended.
- Whether the separation was a resignation, layoff, discharge, or mutual end date.
- Final timesheet, overtime calculation, commission plan, and bonus terms.
- Written PTO or vacation policy in force on the separation date.

What must be included in your final paycheck
Your Kansas employer must include all of the following in your final paycheck:
- ✓All earned wages through your last day (hourly or salary)
- ✓Overtime pay owed for hours worked
- ✓Commission that has already been earned and is calculable
- ✓Any unreimbursed business expenses (where contractually required)
Whether accrued vacation or PTO must be included depends on Kansas's PTO payout law. See Kansas PTO payout rules →
If your employer pays late
- 1. Document your last day worked and what you are owed.
- 2. Confirm whether the deadline changed because you resigned rather than being terminated.
- 3. Send a written demand to your employer; email is usually enough to preserve a record.
- 4. File a wage claim with the state labor agency if payment still does not arrive.
Frequently asked questions
How long does my employer have to give me my final paycheck in Kansas?
In Kansas, the deadline depends on how your employment ended. If your employer terminated you, your final paycheck is due next regular payday. If you resigned voluntarily, it is due next regular payday. These are minimum legal deadlines — your employer can pay sooner, but not later.
What must be included in my final paycheck in Kansas?
Your final paycheck in Kansas must include all wages earned through your last day of work, including any overtime owed and any accrued but unpaid commissions or bonuses that are calculable at the time. Whether accrued PTO must be included depends on Kansas's PTO payout law — check the Kansas employment law page for specifics. Final paychecks cannot have unauthorized deductions.
What can I do if my employer is late with my final paycheck in Kansas?
Kansas gives you enforcement options if the deadline is missed: file with the Kansas Department of Labor (https://www.dol.ks.gov) or sue in civil court. Awards frequently add penalties and attorney fees to the unpaid wages. Preserve evidence — your final day, the outstanding amount, and written exchanges with the employer.
Can my employer deduct money from my final paycheck in Kansas?
Only if the law requires it or you agreed in writing. Kansas employers may deduct taxes and garnishments, but withholding final pay to recover property or a cash advance — without written authorization or court process — is a wage violation you can act on.
Does Kansas have penalties for employers who pay the final paycheck late?
Yes. Kansas provides remedies for employees whose final paycheck is late. Depending on the circumstances, you may be entitled to the unpaid wages plus waiting-time penalties, liquidated damages, or interest. The Kansas labor enforcement agency can investigate your complaint and order payment. You may also pursue a private civil claim in state court. Contact the Kansas Department of Labor at https://www.dol.ks.gov to file a claim.
